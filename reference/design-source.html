Name your
summer.

You only get so many summers. Most of them blur. The ones you remember had names — and the names came from doing things on purpose.

The journey · six minutes

Six steps. Six models.

Each step is named for the psychology research it's built on. Nothing here is decorative.

What this is built on

Three pieces of real research.

Implementation intentions
If-then plans roughly double success rate
94 studies, d ≈ 0.65. Most-studied behavior-change intervention. Gollwitzer & Sheeran, 2006.
Mental contrasting
Pure positive thinking backfires
Fantasy without obstacle predicts worse outcomes. 25 years of replications. Oettingen, 2014.
Anticipatory savoring
Looking forward is its own reward
Vacations: two weeks before Greece beats the week in Greece. Bryant & Veroff, 2007.
What you walk away with

One manifesto. Shareable.

A page with your name, your three if-then plans (illustrated), your anchor event with live countdown, and a quiet line about the obstacle you named — so when it hits, you've already decided what to do.

Step 1 · Fresh start (Naming)

A name creates a line.

No name
"Summer 2022" → blur
With a name
"Summer of saying yes" → every choice has a vote

The cheapest commitment available — ten seconds, costs nothing, gives every later choice a measure to vote against.

Source: Fresh-start effect · Dai, Milkman & Riis, 2014, Management Science

Step 3 · Contrasting (Get honest)

Wishing alone backfires.

Wish only
Brain rewards you now for outcome that hasn't happened →
predicts worse outcomes
Wish + obstacle
Daydream gets contrasted against the real friction →
predicts action

Name the friction. It's almost never money or time — it's a habit. Yours, at 7pm on the couch.

Source: Mental contrasting · Oettingen, 2014 · 25 years of replication

Step 4 · Pre-commitment (Quit-date hobby)

Pre-commit to stop.

Open-ended
"I'm becoming a chess player"
→ pressure, never starts
Time-boxed
"I'm playing chess for the summer"
→ starts tonight

September 21 is locked. Autumn equinox. The day this hobby ends, no matter what. Knowing the end is coming makes starting weightless.

Source: Pre-commitment lowers activation cost · behavioral economics 101

Pre-decided triggers bypass willpower. When the moment comes, you don't deliberate — you execute the plan you already made. The trigger must be specific: a time, a place, a moment. Not a feeling.

Source: Implementation intentions · Gollwitzer & Sheeran, 2006 meta-analysis

Step 6 · Anticipation (Anchor event)

Looking forward is the reward.

"The two weeks before Greece generate more cumulative happiness than the week actually in Greece."

— Bryant & Veroff, Savoring (2007)

One specific dated event. Specificity is what lets the brain rehearse it. "Train to Lisbon, July 14" beats "summer travel."

Source: Anchored anticipation · Bryant & Veroff, 2007 · Van Boven, 2010
