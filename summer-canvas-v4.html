Step 1 of 6 · Identity

What's this summer going to be called?

Naming creates a mental boundary between your past self and your summer self. "The summer of ___" is a verbal commitment. (Dai, Milkman & Riis, 2014.)

The summer of speed The summer of saying yes The summer of the lake The summer of slow mornings
Step 2 of 6 · Imagine forward

It's late August. You're glowing. What do you tell a friend?

Picture sitting across from a friend in September. They ask how summer was. You light up. Imagining future positive moments in sensory detail generates real positive emotion right now — and increases enjoyment later. (Bryant & Veroff, 2007.)

Step 3 of 6 · Get honest

What's most likely to get in the way?

This is the step most goal-setting skips. Positive fantasies alone predict worse outcomes — you need to name the obstacle in the same breath as the wish. It's almost always internal. (Oettingen.)

Step 4 of 6 · Low-stakes start

One thing you'll start now and quit on September 21.

A hobby with a built-in end date removes performance pressure. You're not getting back into chess — you're getting back into chess for the summer. Pre-committing to stop makes starting easy.

Step 5 of 6 · The engine

Three if-then plans. This is the part that works.

Meta-analysis of 94 studies: if-then plans roughly double the rate of goal attainment (d ≈ 0.65). The trigger has to be specific — a time, a place, a moment — not a feeling. (Gollwitzer & Sheeran, 2006.)

Step 6 of 6 · Anchor

One specific thing. One specific date.

Anticipation is a generator of present positive affect. Naming one dated event you already look forward to gives you something to mentally rehearse for the next 90 days. ("Train to Lisbon, July 14" beats "summer travel.")
